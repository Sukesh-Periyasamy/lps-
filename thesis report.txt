1. Materials
Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) samples from Gram-negative bacteria were used in this study. LPS samples were either obtained commercially (e.g., from Sigma-Aldrich) or extracted from bacterial cultures using standard protocols.

Control biomolecules, including peptidoglycan (PGN) and lipoteichoic acid (LTA), were used for comparative analysis. Deionized water was used throughout all experiments.

2. Extraction and Purification of LPS
LPS samples were extracted from bacterial cells using the hot phenol–water extraction method. The extracted samples were subjected to:

Dialysis to remove impurities
Freeze-drying for concentration
Ethanol washing to eliminate residual phospholipids
This process yielded purified LPS suitable for spectroscopic analysis.

3. Preparation of SERS Substrate
Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) substrates were prepared using silver nanostructures. Typically, silver nanorod (AgNR) arrays were fabricated on clean glass slides under vacuum deposition conditions.

These substrates provide high enhancement factors due to localized surface plasmon resonance, enabling detection of low-concentration biomolecules.

4. Sample Preparation
LPS samples were prepared at a concentration of approximately 100 μg/mL in deionized water.

A small volume (2 μL) of the sample was dispensed onto the SERS substrate
The sample was allowed to air dry at room temperature (~20°C)
The dried sample formed a thin layer suitable for Raman measurement
5. Raman/SERS Measurement
Raman measurements were carried out using a confocal Raman microscope with the following parameters:

Excitation wavelength: 785 nm laser
Laser power: ~9 mW
Acquisition time: ~10 seconds
Objective lens: low magnification (e.g., 5×)
The SERS technique enabled detection of LPS at very low quantities (~picogram level).

6. Data Collection
Multiple spectra were collected from different regions of the substrate to ensure reproducibility.

Several spectra per sample were recorded
Outliers (noise, spikes) were removed
Final dataset consisted of hundreds of spectra per sample
7. Data Pre-processing
The raw Raman spectra were pre-processed to improve quality and consistency:

Baseline correction to remove background signals

 Final Conclusion
This study focused on the spectroscopic analysis and identification of lipopolysaccharide (LPS) using Raman spectroscopy, supported by Density Functional Theory (DFT) modeling and literature-based validation. LPS, a major component of the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria, plays a critical role in bacterial pathogenicity, particularly through its lipid A region. Due to its structural complexity, direct quantum mechanical modeling of the entire LPS molecule is not feasible, necessitating the use of representative molecular fragments.

The experimental Raman/SERS analysis revealed that LPS exhibits a distinct spectral fingerprint characterized by multiple vibrational bands. Prominent peaks were observed in regions corresponding to carbohydrate (~1000 cm⁻¹), lipid (~1300–1450 cm⁻¹), and higher-frequency interaction modes (~1600 cm⁻¹). These peaks arise primarily from the combined contributions of lipid A and core oligosaccharide components. However, it was observed that individual peaks are not exclusive to LPS and may overlap with other biomolecules such as peptidoglycan (PGN) and lipoteichoic acid (LTA). Therefore, reliable identification of LPS requires analysis of the overall spectral pattern rather than reliance on a single peak.

Comparative analysis demonstrated that LPS possesses a more complex and feature-rich spectrum than Gram-positive biomolecules. While PGN and LTA exhibit fewer characteristic peaks and simpler spectral profiles, LPS shows multiple distinct bands due to its combined lipid and carbohydrate composition. This difference enables differentiation of LPS from other bacterial components based on Raman spectral fingerprints.

The application of Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) significantly improved detection sensitivity, enabling the identification of LPS at extremely low concentrations (picogram level). Furthermore, integration with machine learning techniques allowed highly accurate classification of spectral data. Classical models such as Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), and Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) achieved accuracies exceeding 99%, while the deep learning model RamanNet demonstrated perfect classification accuracy (100%). These results highlight the potential of combining Raman spectroscopy with advanced data analysis techniques for rapid and reliable biomolecular detection.

From a theoretical perspective, DFT calculations performed on representative molecular fragments such as Kdo and related sugar units provide valuable insight into vibrational modes. Although full LPS modeling is computationally prohibitive, fragment-based approaches successfully capture the essential spectral features observed experimentally. The correlation between experimental Raman peaks and theoretical vibrational assignments confirms the validity of this approach.

Overall, this study demonstrates that Raman spectroscopy, particularly when enhanced by SERS and combined with DFT modeling and machine learning, provides a powerful framework for the identification and analysis of LPS. The findings establish that LPS can be reliably distinguished based on its molecular vibrational fingerprint, despite spectral overlap with other biomolecules.
